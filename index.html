Shuffling Cards

Riffling to randomness

By: Fred Hohman

Most people have seen someone shuffle a deck of cards. Sometimes you split a deck in half and switch halves. Sometimes you smoosh them all around on a table until they are mixed up. But many people shuffle using a riffle:

Here’s a question for you: How many times do you have to riffle a deck of cards before it is completely shuffled?

Luckily, mathematics has you covered. It's seven. Riffle seven times and you will have a completely random ordering of cards, one that likely has never been the same before.

I found this result from a 2015 Numberphile video, in which a number of interesting card shuffling facts are presented. However, one caught my attention. Instead of riffling with two halves of the deck, what if you were to only riffle 1 card at a time? That is, using a standard 52 card deck, in one hand hold 51 cards and in the other hold the remaining single card. Now riffle these together. This is equivalent to just taking one card and placing it at random inside of the deck. So here’s the question:

How many single riffles do you have to do in order to have a completely shuffled deck?

Theorem

You could simulate this in a short program, but instead we can solve it exactly using math. Consider an order deck of cards. Without loss of generality, let’s say the suits are in the following order: ♠, ♣, ♥, ♦. So our deck looks like this.

So the bottom suit is ♦, which means the bottom card of our deck is the King of Diamonds (K♦). Now do the following iteration:

Place the top card of the deck randomly inside the deck

In our example above, we would take the A♠ and place it randomly somewhere in the deck. Our new top card is the 2♠. If we continue this procedure of taking the top card and randomly placing somewhere else in the deck, eventually we’ll move the top card to the very bottom of the deck, which means our K♦ is now the 51st card in the deck from the top. Since for every top card you place has a 152\frac{1}{52}521​ chance of going to the bottom, that means, on average, after about 52 top card riffles, the top card will go underneath the K♦.

Now, if we do another top card riffle, there are now two spots to put the top card underneath the K♦, i.e., a 252\frac{2}{52}522​ chance of going underneath.

Note: notice the K♦ can only rise in the deck. There are two cases:

  1. The top card is placed above the K♦, therefore its position does not change.
  2. The top card is placed underneath the K♦, therefore it rises one position closer to the top.

If we continue this process, the K♦ will eventually become the top card of our deck. Once this last card from our oriignal ordered deck is shuffled into the deck, we now have a completely shuffled deck, that is, this order is equally likely to any othe order.

So how many single card riffles does this take? Recall each time a card is placed underneath the K♦, out chances of placing another card increases. Following the logic from above, we can calculate the number of riffles it would take.

∑i=15252i=521+522+523+...+5252≈235\sum_{i=1}^{52} \frac{52}{i} = \frac{52}{1} + \frac{52}{2} + \frac{52}{3} + ... + \frac{52}{52} \approx 235∑i=152​i52​=152​+252​+352​+...+5252​≈235

That means, on average, after 235 single card riffles, we will have a completely randomly shuffled deck of cards.
