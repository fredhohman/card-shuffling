Shuffling Cards

Riffling to randomness

By: Fred Hohman

Most people have seen a deck of cards be shuffled. Sometimes the deck is split in half and the halves are switched. Sometimes the deck is smooshed around on a table until they are all mixed up. But most of the time, a deck of cards is shuffled using a riffle.

Here’s a question for you: How many times do you have to riffle a deck of cards before it is completely shuffled? Luckily, mathematics has us covered. It's seven. Riffle seven times and we will have a a sufficient random ordering of cards, an ordering that has likely never been the same before.

This math paper also shows a number of other interesting card suffling results, in all its gory details.

I found this result from a 2015 Numberphile video, in which a number of interesting card shuffling facts are presented. However, one caught my attention. Instead of a standard riffle using a deck roughly splitin half, what if you were to only riffle 1 card at a time? That is, using a standard deck of 52 cards, in one hand hold we would hold 51 cards and in the other hold the remaining single card. Now riffle these together. This is equivalent to taking one card and placing it at random inside of the deck.

So here’s the question:
How many single riffles do you have to do in order to have a completely shuffled deck?

Theorem

You could simulate this in a short program, which we will do towards the end, but first, we can solve for the number of riffles explicitly.

Consider an ordered deck of cards. Without loss of generality, let’s say the suits are in the following order: ♠, ♣, ♥, ♦. So our ordered deck looks like this.

The bottom suit is ♦, which means the bottom card of our deck is the King of Diamonds (K♦). Now do the following iteration:

Place the top card of the deck randomly inside the deck

In our case, we would take the A♠ and place it randomly somewhere in the deck. Our new top card then becomes 2♠. If we continue this procedure of taking the top card and randomly placing somewhere else in the deck, eventually we’ll place the top card at the very bottom of the deck, which means our original bottom card, the K♦, is now the penultimate card in the deck, i.e., the 51st card from the top. Since for every top card we move has a 152\frac{1}{52}521​ chance of moving to any new position in the deck, that means, on average, after about 52 top card riffles, the top card will become the new bottom card.

Now, once the original bottom card, the K♦, moves up one potision, if we do another top card riffle, there will now be two spots the new top card can be palced that is underneath the K♦, i.e., a 152+152=252\frac{1}{52}+\frac{1}{52}=\frac{2}{52}521​+521​=522​ chance of going underneath.

Note: notice the K♦ can only rise in the deck. There are two cases:

  1. The top card is placed above the K♦, therefore its position does not change.
  2. The top card is placed underneath the K♦, therefore it rises one position closer to the top.

If we continue this procedure, our original bottom card, the K♦, will eventually become the top card of our deck. Once this last card from our oriignal ordered deck is shuffled into the deck, we now have a randomly shuffled deck, that is, this deck’s order is equally likely to any other order.

So, how many single card riffles does this take? Recall each time a card is placed underneath the K♦, out chances of placing another card increases by 152\frac{1}{52}521​ . Therefore, we can calculate the number of riffles it would take.

∑i=15252i=521+522+523+...+5252≈235\sum_{i=1}^{52} \frac{52}{i} = \frac{52}{1} + \frac{52}{2} + \frac{52}{3} + ... + \frac{52}{52} \approx 235∑i=152​i52​=152​+252​+352​+...+5252​≈235

That means, on average, after 235 single card riffles, we will have a randomly shuffled deck of cards.
